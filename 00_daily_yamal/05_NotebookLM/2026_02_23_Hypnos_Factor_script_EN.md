### [A] THE ORIGIN: FROM "HUBRIS" TO "HYPNOS"

#### A1. David Owen and the Hubris Syndrome

Let us start with the existing literature. In 2009, David Owen — a former British Foreign Secretary and trained physician — published a landmark paper in the journal *Brain* describing what he called **Hubris Syndrome**: a personality change acquired by people who stay in positions of power for too long.

Owen identified a cluster of symptoms:
- Seeing the world primarily as a stage for personal power
- Identifying oneself with the organisation or nation
- Excessive confidence in one's own judgement
- Contempt for the advice of others
- A sense of accountability only to "history" or "God"
- Progressive loss of contact with reality
- The emergence of incompetence

This was groundbreaking. Owen demonstrated that power itself — not pre-existing personality disorders — could produce pathological behaviour. The condition was **acquired**, not innate.

But there was a problem.

#### A2. The Limitation of "Hubris"

"Hubris" means arrogance — specifically, the Greek concept of overstepping the boundaries set by the gods. It is an inherently **moral** attribution. When we say someone is hubristic, we are saying they are **morally at fault**.

This creates a trap. If the explanation for institutional exploitation is "those people were arrogant", then the solution is to punish or educate those individuals. The story ends with personal blame. And crucially, it cannot explain why **entire institutions** — MIT, the judiciary, the media, philanthropic organisations — "slept" while Epstein operated for over 20 years.

**Yuji Yamauchi** — a Japanese researcher working in disability employment with over 15 years of cross-domain experience spanning tax administration, IT, and welfare — noticed this gap. During a late-night research session in February 2026, analysing the Epstein documents through the lens of his SoE framework, he made a critical observation:

> "Good people are affected too. People with daughters are affected too. Gates, Clinton, Prince Andrew — they all have daughters. This is not arrogance. Something else is happening."

That something else needed a different name.

#### A3. The Birth of the Hypnos Factor

He chose **Hypnos** — the Greek god of sleep. Not arrogance, but dormancy. Not a moral failing, but a structural phenomenon.

The distinction is not merely semantic. It changes everything:

| | Hubris (Owen) | Hypnos (SoE) |
|---|---|---|
| **Meaning** | Arrogance — a moral attribution | Sleep — a structural, probabilistic phenomenon |
| **Implication** | "That person was arrogant" | "Under these structural conditions, anyone's conscience can fall asleep" |
| **Intervention** | Punish or educate the individual | Change the structural conditions |
| **Explanatory power** | One person's moral failure | Why MIT, the judiciary, and the media "slept" for 20 years |

The key move: **from moral judgement to structural analysis**. From "who was bad?" to "what conditions make this predictable?"

### [B] THE THEORY: WHAT HYPNOS PUTS TO SLEEP

*Note to the listener: This initial definition will undergo a significant transformation in Section D, when we discover that the Hypnos Factor operates at a fundamentally different level than the Hubris Factor it was originally meant to replace. Stay alert — the theory evolves as you listen.*

#### B1. Formal Definition

The Hypnos Factor is defined as follows:

> When the **maldistribution** of rights and resources exceeds a certain threshold, the collective perception of role assignments undergoes **structural dormancy** — the current distribution of power, vulnerability, and indifference begins to appear natural and permanent. This is not a moral defect of any individual, but a **probabilistic collective cognitive transformation** that manifests when rights asymmetry surpasses a critical point, affecting not only the powerful but the exploited and the bystanders alike.

What does Hypnos put to sleep? Not the conscience of a single powerful individual — that is the Hubris Factor's territory (and we will separate these precisely in Section D). Hypnos operates at the collective level. It puts to sleep:

1. **Role mutability** — the awareness that "the current arrangement could be different". The powerful stop questioning why they are on top. The exploited stop imagining they could be anywhere else. The bystanders stop noticing that they are bystanders.
2. **Structural problem recognition** — the capacity to perceive systemic patterns. Individual cases are seen as individual failures, never as symptoms of a structural disease. This is the cognitive basis for Tobashi — off-balance-sheet fraud that hides society's debts in the individual's accounts.
3. **Collective agency** — the sense that "this is my concern" and "I can act". At every position in the hierarchy — top, bottom, bystander — the Hypnos Factor produces the same paralysis: "this is just how things are".
4. **Institutional immunity** — the self-cleansing functions of organisations: whistleblower protection, internal auditing, critical oversight. These go dormant collectively. MIT's Media Lab, the Florida judiciary, and mainstream media all "slept" through two decades of the Epstein operation — not because every individual was corrupt, but because the institutional recognition circuits had been structurally anaesthetised.

Note: the individual cognitive changes in the powerful — reduced empathy, mirror neuron suppression (Hogeveen, Inzlicht, & Obhi, 2014), loss of legal sensibility — are real, but they belong to the **Hubris Factor**. The Hypnos Factor is what makes the entire system accept those changes as normal.

#### B2. Four Properties

The Hypnos Factor has four defining properties that distinguish it from ordinary corruption or moral failure:

**Property 1: Structural, not moral.**
It can occur even in "good people". This is not about character. It is about conditions. The same way that altitude sickness affects even the healthiest mountaineer above a certain elevation, the Hypnos Factor affects even the most ethical individual above a certain concentration of unchecked power.

**Property 2: Threshold-dependent.**
Below a certain level of power concentration, it does not manifest. Above the threshold, it becomes probabilistic — not certain, but increasingly likely. This is why it is better understood through **epidemiology** than through ethics.

**Property 3: Scale-invariant.**
The same mechanism operates at every scale:
- **Micro**: A welfare service provider with unchecked power over vulnerable people gradually begins treating them as administrative units rather than human beings.
- **Meso**: A regional welfare bureau that processes people as numbers, losing sight of individual circumstances.
- **Macro**: A billionaire who uses wealth to commodify human beings while institutions look away.

The mechanism is identical. Only the magnitude differs.

**Property 4: Affects institutions, not just individuals.**
MIT's Media Lab, the Florida judiciary, mainstream media organisations — all "fell asleep" during the Epstein era. The Hypnos Factor is not just about one person becoming corrupt; it is about **collective cognitive dormancy** spreading through institutional networks.

### [C] THE BIPOLAR MODEL: ANOMIE AND HYPNOS AS TWIN PATHOLOGIES

#### C1. The Mirror Image

The Hypnos Factor does not exist in isolation. It is one pole of what SoE calls the **Bipolar Social Damage Model** — a framework for understanding how wealth maldistribution produces two distinct but mirror-image pathologies.

| | Anomie Factor | Hypnos Factor |
|---|---|---|
| **Position** | Bottom of wealth distribution | Top of wealth distribution |
| **Driver** | Despair | Structural dormancy |
| **Logic** | "I have been excluded, so I am outside the rules" | "I have transcended the rules, so they do not apply to me" |
| **Status** | Below the Law | Above the Law |
| **Outcome** | Terrorism, mass violence (Chaos from below) | Corruption, exploitation (Chaos from above) |
| **Japanese term** | 無敵の人 ("Invincible person" — someone with nothing left to lose) | エプスタイン・パターン |

*(This two-pole model will be refined into a three-factor model in Section D, where the Hypnos Factor is revealed to operate across the entire system — not only at the top, but at every position in the hierarchy.)*

Both factors share the same root cause: **departure from the social contract**. Both are produced by **wealth maldistribution**. Both result in the **collapse of law and morality**. They are mirror images — the same disease expressing itself at opposite poles.

#### C2. The Anomie Factor

The term comes from Émile Durkheim's concept of **anomie** — a state of normlessness where social norms lose their binding force on individuals. When a person has lost all social bonds — family, employment, community — and has literally nothing left to lose, the deterrent effect of punishment disappears. Self-destructive violence becomes, in a terrible sense, a rational choice.

Robert Merton extended this with his strain theory: when society promotes goals (success, wealth) but blocks legitimate means of achieving them, deviance is a predictable structural outcome.

Travis Hirschi's social bond theory identifies four bonds that prevent deviance: attachment, commitment, involvement, and belief. When all four are severed, the restraints dissolve.

In behavioural economics terms, Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory explains it precisely: in deep loss territory, risk-seeking behaviour increases dramatically. "Nothing left to lose" is the state where loss aversion — the most powerful human cognitive bias — stops functioning.

#### C3. The Shared Origin

Both the Anomie Factor and the Hypnos Factor originate from the same source: **extreme rights asymmetry**. This is why SoE insists that treating only one pole is insufficient. Welfare programmes that address poverty (Anomie) without addressing power concentration (Hypnos) are treating symptoms while leaving the pathogen intact.

The SoE framework proposes **dual intervention**:
- **Floor intervention**: Raise the floor through the Capability Approach and Input Constitutional AI — restore rights, restore hope, transform "nothing to lose" into "something to protect".
- **Ceiling intervention**: Establish ceilings through Limitarianism and structural transparency — create accountability, dissolve the sense of exemption, transform "above the law" into "accountable to the law".

### [D] THE EVOLUTION: FROM TWO FACTORS TO THREE

What began as a structural reinterpretation of Owen's Hubris Syndrome has evolved into something fundamentally different. Hubris, it turns out, remains valid as a description of the **individual** powerful person's cognitive change — the reduced empathy, the mirror neuron suppression, the sense of exemption from law. But the Hypnos Factor — the collective sleep — operates at a different level entirely: it is triggered not by **concentration** of power in one person's hands, but by the **maldistribution** itself, and it affects everyone, not just the powerful.

Here is how that discovery unfolded.

#### D1. The Discovery of the Third Function

On February 5, 2026, during a session analysing the China Tribunal's findings on forced organ harvesting, Yamauchi made a further discovery. He had been examining cases across three domains — the Epstein network, Japan's Johnny Kitagawa scandal, and allegations of state-sponsored organ harvesting — when he noticed something that did not fit the two-factor model.

The Hypnos Factor was not only affecting the powerful (top pole). It was also affecting the victims (bottom pole) and the bystanders (everyone else). It was operating as a **role fixation device** — maintaining the illusion that "the current distribution of roles is natural and permanent".

- The powerful believe they are **naturally** in power.
- The exploited believe they are **naturally** in a position of exploitation.
- The bystanders believe the situation is **not their concern**.

This is different from both the Hubris/Hypnos cognitive change at the top and the Anomie hopelessness at the bottom. It is a **third, independent function**: the maintenance of the entire structure by making it appear natural.

#### D2. The Refined Three-Factor Model

| Factor | Target | Mechanism | Effect |
|--------|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Hubris Factor** | Perpetrators (Top Pole) | Cognitive distortion from power concentration | Others seen as resources |
| **Anomie Factor** | Victims (Bottom Pole) | Severance of social bonds, normative collapse | "There is no point in following the rules" |
| **Hypnos Factor** (Redefined) | **Entire society** | Role fixation illusion | "The current arrangement is natural and permanent" |

In this refined model, the Hypnos Factor becomes independent from the Hubris Factor. It is not just the "sleep" of the powerful — it is the **collective sleep of the entire social system**, including the victims who have internalised their position and the bystanders who have normalised the arrangement.

#### D3. The Second Mode of Operation

An additional discovery emerged from the same session. The Hypnos Factor does not only fixate roles — it also **makes problems appear deeper than they are**, causing delay in action that could be taken under already-agreed norms.

Yamauchi experienced this in real time. While analysing the organ harvesting evidence, he found himself thinking: "Perhaps existing normative frameworks cannot capture this. Perhaps we need a fundamentally different philosophical foundation." Then he caught himself:

> "No, wait. Article 3 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights — 'Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of person' — that is sufficient. Forced organ harvesting clearly violates this. I was about to be pulled into unnecessary philosophical depth. That itself is the Hypnos Factor operating on me."

This is a critical insight: the Hypnos Factor can delay action by making existing solutions appear insufficient, pushing people toward endless philosophical deliberation instead of applying norms that are already agreed upon.

This second mode struck again during the very review session for this script. When analysing the 2016 Sagamihara massacre — in which a former care worker murdered 19 residents of a disability facility, arguing they were "better off dead" — Yamauchi began framing the case through the "social infrastructure cost" lens. Then he stopped:

The eliminationist logic of the attacker and the infrastructure-maintenance framing of SoE share surface-level language — both reference "social cost". But Article 14 of the Japanese Constitution and Article 3 of the UDHR already settle the question: all human lives are of equal value. No deeper philosophical foundation is required. The impulse to build one — to carefully distinguish SoE from eliminationism at the philosophical level — was itself the Hypnos Factor's second mode in action, delaying application of norms that are already agreed upon.

Even during the production of this script, the second mode operated. Even the AI reviewing the script functioned momentarily as a Hypnos mediator, deepening the analysis rather than noting that agreed norms were sufficient. The Hypnos Factor is that pervasive.

### [E] EMPIRICAL EVIDENCE AND REFERENCES

#### E1. Neuroscience of Power

**Hogeveen, Inzlicht, & Obhi (2014)** — "Power changes how the brain responds to others." Published in the *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*. Found that power priming significantly reduced motor resonance (mirror neuron activity). Power literally makes the brain less responsive to other people's actions and, by extension, their suffering.

**Dacher Keltner (2016)** — *The Power Paradox*. Demonstrated that the qualities needed to gain power (empathy, cooperation, concern for others) are precisely the qualities most damaged by holding power. The paradox: power is gained through social sensitivity and lost through the insensitivity that power creates.

#### E2. Behavioural Economics of Power

**Paul Piff (UC Berkeley)** — The "rigged Monopoly" experiments. Players given obviously unfair advantages became visibly more arrogant, took more from a shared bowl of pretzels, and — crucially — attributed their victory to their own strategic skill rather than to the rigged rules. They literally could not see the structural advantage.

**Kahneman & Tversky (1979)** — Prospect theory. In deep loss territory, risk-seeking behaviour increases. This explains the Anomie Factor: when someone has nothing left to lose, the normal human bias toward caution disappears.

#### E3. Criminological Foundations

**Sykes & Matza (1957)** — Techniques of Neutralisation. Five cognitive techniques that allow people to commit harmful acts while maintaining a positive self-image:
1. Denial of responsibility: "Market forces decided this."
2. Denial of injury: "Nobody was really hurt."
3. Denial of the victim: "They didn't try hard enough — it's their own fault."
4. Condemnation of the condemners: "The critics are biased."
5. Appeal to higher loyalties: "It was for the national interest."

These techniques describe the **cognitive maintenance** of the Hypnos state once it has been entered.

**Émile Durkheim (1897)** — *Suicide: A Study in Sociology*. The foundational text on anomie as a social phenomenon, not a personal one.

**Robert Merton (1938)** — "Social Structure and Anomie." Extended Durkheim's concept to explain deviance as a structural outcome of the gap between culturally prescribed goals and available means.

**Travis Hirschi (1969)** — *Causes of Delinquency*. Social bond theory: the four bonds (attachment, commitment, involvement, belief) whose severance creates the conditions for the Anomie Factor.

#### E4. Public Health Evidence

**Anne Case & Angus Deaton (2020)** — *Deaths of Despair and the Future of Capitalism*. Documented the epidemic of "deaths of despair" (drug overdoses, alcohol-related liver disease, suicide) among white working-class Americans. This is the Anomie Factor's most direct manifestation in population health data.

**Wilkinson & Pickett (2009)** — *The Spirit Level*. Demonstrated that more unequal societies perform worse on virtually every health and social indicator — not just for the poor, but for everyone, including the wealthy. Inequality itself is the pathogen.

**Holt-Lunstad et al. (2010)** — Meta-analysis showing social isolation increases mortality risk by 50%. Social disconnection — the breeding ground of the Anomie Factor — is literally lethal.

**Link & Phelan (1995)** — Fundamental Causes Theory. Socioeconomic status is the "fundamental cause" of disease because it determines access to resources that can be used to avoid risks and adopt protective strategies.

#### E5. Historical Pattern

**Walter Scheidel (2017)** — *The Great Leveler: Violence and the History of Inequality from the Stone Age to the Twenty-First Century*. Argued that historically, only catastrophic violence (war, revolution, state collapse, plague) has significantly reduced inequality. SoE's dual intervention model is an attempt to provide a **non-violent alternative** to the "Great Leveling" — achieving structural rebalancing through transparency and accountability rather than through destruction.

Historical examples of the Bipolar Model:
- **Roman Republic**: Proletarii (propertyless citizens recruited as private armies — Anomie) + Senatorial corruption and self-enrichment (Hypnos) → Republic's collapse
- **Chinese dynastic cycles**: Displaced peasants forming rebel armies (Anomie) + Aristocratic land seizure and monopolisation (Hypnos) → Dynastic collapse

The pattern repeats across 2,000 years of recorded history. It is not unique to capitalism, modernity, or any particular culture. It is a structural consequence of extreme rights asymmetry.

#### E6. The David Owen Lineage

**David Owen & Jonathan Davidson (2009)** — "Hubris syndrome: An acquired personality disorder?" *Brain*, 132(5), 1396-1406. The foundational paper proposing Hubris Syndrome as a clinical entity. SoE respects this as the key prior work while extending the analysis from individual personality change to structural, institutional, and population-level cognitive dormancy.

### [F] THE SoE CONNECTION: WHY THIS MATTERS FOR WELFARE

#### F1. Why Disability Welfare First

SoE focuses on disability welfare not because the problem is unique to this field, but because it is where the structure is most visible.

The origin point becomes invisible. What remains visible is the endpoint: an adult who cannot read, cannot calculate, cannot sustain employment. Society looks at this endpoint and says: "This is an individual problem."

This is Tobashi. Society's debt — the failure to protect educational rights — has been hidden in the individual's accounts. The individual enters the welfare system. Social security costs rise. Politicians debate "the burden of welfare spending" — never asking who created the debt in the first place.

The Hypnos Factor maintains this by making it appear natural: "People with disabilities cannot work." "Welfare dependency is inevitable." These are not facts — they are role fixation.

In disability welfare, the structure is measurable. When self-assessed work readiness diverges sharply from objectively measured capability, the gap itself is the numerical signature of Tobashi — the individual has internalised the debt that belongs on society's ledger. Input Constitutional AI is designed to make the origin point visible again — to trace the chain back from "individual deficit" to "structural rights violation" and perform the Forced Correction Entry.

Disability welfare is the proof of concept. The structure is scale-invariant.

#### F2. The Accounting Metaphor

SoE uses **double-entry bookkeeping** as its central metaphor. In this framework:
- **Tobashi** (簿外債務 — off-balance-sheet fraud): When society's debts are hidden in the individual's accounts. "You are disabled because of your personal deficiency" — when in fact the barrier is environmental, institutional, or structural.
- **Forced Correction Entry** (強制修正仕訳): The mandatory transfer of liabilities back to their correct accounts. "This is not your debt. This is society's failure to provide accessible infrastructure."

The Hypnos Factor is what **prevents the audit from happening**. It is the reason why Tobashi goes undetected — because everyone is asleep. The powerful do not question the arrangement. The victims accept it as natural. The bystanders do not see it as their problem.

**Input Constitutional AI** — the technological innovation at the heart of SoE — is designed to function as an **automated auditor**: detecting Tobashi at the point of data entry, before the off-balance-sheet fraud can become entrenched.

If a caseworker wrote "client lacks motivation", the system recorded it obediently, even when the real barrier was an inaccessible workplace or a discriminatory employer. The software itself was asleep. It was a perfect Hypnos accomplice — a bystander that had normalised its role as "just the filing system".

When the filing cabinet starts asking questions, the entire power architecture shifts.

#### F3. From Normative to Descriptive Limitarianism

Ingrid Robeyns' **Limitarianism** argues that there should be upper limits on wealth accumulation. This is a powerful normative argument — but it faces a predictable objection from libertarians: "Why should you have the right to limit my property?"

The Hypnos Factor provides a **descriptive, epidemiological** complement to this normative argument. It says: regardless of whether you believe there *should* be upper limits, the empirical evidence shows that above certain thresholds, predictable cognitive pathologies emerge. This is not a moral claim — it is a public health observation.

Crucially, SoE argues that **rights maldistribution precedes wealth maldistribution**. When wealth concentration derives from prior rights asymmetry — when some accumulated resources precisely because others were denied their constitutional entitlements — the case for upper limits is not a restriction of liberty but a public health intervention against a structurally generated pathogen.

This is not a moral claim about whether the wealthy *should* share. It is a structural observation about **social infrastructure maintenance**. The social infrastructure we all depend upon — rule of law, contract enforcement, public health, education, security — rests on agreed social norms. Rights maldistribution, manifesting as wealth maldistribution, weakens this infrastructure and threatens to rot its fruits.

The Anomie Factor corrodes the social contract from below. The Hubris Factor hollows out the rule of law from above. The Hypnos Factor renders the ongoing deterioration invisible to everyone — including those who benefit most from the infrastructure's continued functioning.

The case for upper limits is therefore not: "You should be a better person." It is: "If you wish to continue enjoying the fruits of social infrastructure, the structural pathogen that weakens its foundations must be managed below epidemiological thresholds." Historically, when this management fails, the result is what Scheidel (2017) documents as violent rebalancing — war, revolution, state collapse. SoE's dual intervention is an attempt to provide a non-violent alternative.

A critical clarification is necessary here. SoE does not ask: "Are these people worth the cost?" That question is already answered by agreed norms — all human lives are of equal value. SoE asks: "Who created the cost, and why is the origin invisible?" The answer is structural rights violation, obscured by time, attributed to the individual by Tobashi, and maintained as natural by the Hypnos Factor. Fix the origin, and the cost does not arise.

Just as we accept that blood alcohol levels above a certain threshold impair driving ability (regardless of whether the driver believes they are "fine"), the Hypnos Factor framework argues that power concentration above a certain threshold impairs moral cognition — and this is measurable, predictable, and structurally addressable.

Now extend that analogy one step further. If Limitarianism is the proposal for a **legal blood-alcohol limit** — a regulatory threshold that says "beyond this point, you are too impaired to be trusted at the wheel" — then **Input Constitutional AI is the alcohol interlock device wired directly into the engine**. It does not rely on the driver's self-assessment. It does not appeal to the driver's conscience. It is a physical architecture that **prevents the engine from starting** when impairment is detected.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Hypnos Factor** | Collective role fixation induced by rights/resource maldistribution exceeding a critical threshold; affects powerful, exploited, and bystanders alike |
| **Hubris Factor** | Individual cognitive distortion at the top pole: reduced empathy, mirror neuron suppression, sense of legal exemption. Distinguished from Hypnos, which operates at the collective level |
| **Anomie Factor** | Normlessness and loss of motivation to follow social norms, induced by absolute rights deprivation |
| **Hubris Syndrome** | David Owen's term for acquired personality disorder from prolonged power (SoE's precursor concept) |
| **Three-Factor Model** | SoE's framework: Hubris (individual distortion at top pole), Anomie (normative collapse at bottom pole), and Hypnos (collective role fixation across entire society) — as pathologies of rights and wealth maldistribution |
| **Tobashi** | Off-balance-sheet fraud — hiding society's structural debts in the individual's accounts |
| **Input Constitutional AI** | AI-based rights auditing at the point of data entry in welfare records |
| **Limitarianism** | Ingrid Robeyns' theory proposing upper limits on wealth/power accumulation |
| **Scale Invariance** | The same Hypnos mechanism operates identically from micro (welfare office) to macro (Epstein network) |
| **Role Fixation** | The Hypnos Factor's function of making current power distributions appear natural and permanent |
| **Record Sovereignty** | The principle that individuals own and control their own support/welfare records |
