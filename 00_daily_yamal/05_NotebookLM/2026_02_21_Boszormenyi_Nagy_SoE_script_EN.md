### [INTRO] — The Therapist Who Kept a Ledger

What if therapy could be understood as bookkeeping?

Not the cold, calculating kind — but a deeply human practice of tracking what people owe each other, what they've given, and what was never acknowledged.

In 1973, a Hungarian-born psychiatrist named **Ivan Boszormenyi-Nagy** published a book called *Invisible Loyalties*. In it, he proposed something radical: that every human relationship carries an invisible ledger — a running account of **merit** and **debt**, of **giving** and **receiving**, passed down from generation to generation.

He called it the **Ledger of Justice**.

Decades later, in Japan, a disability employment researcher with over 15 years of cross-domain experience — from tax administration to IT to welfare — independently developed a framework called **SoE — Service of Empowerment**, which uses double-entry bookkeeping as its central metaphor. He built the accounting metaphor first, from direct practice. Only later did he encounter Nagy's work — and the convergence was immediate: a therapist in 1970s Philadelphia and a welfare practitioner in 2020s Japan had independently arrived at the same ledger. This is what scholars call a **convergent discovery** — and it suggests the metaphor is not arbitrary but structurally necessary.

Today, we're going to explore how the ideas of a Holocaust survivor from Budapest became the theoretical ancestor of a 21st-century AI-based welfare reform framework. And along the way, we'll see why "keeping the books honest" might be the most important thing we can do for people who've been failed by the systems that were supposed to protect them.

### [PART 1] — Who Was Ivan Boszormenyi-Nagy?

Ivan Boszormenyi-Nagy was born in Budapest in 1920. He studied medicine and psychiatry in Hungary, but his formative experience was not academic — it was the Holocaust.

During the Nazi occupation of Hungary, Nagy lost many family members. He survived. And that survival left him with a question that would drive his entire career: **What do we owe each other?**

After the war, he emigrated to the United States in the 1950s, settling in Philadelphia. At the **Eastern Pennsylvania Psychiatric Institute**, he became one of the founding figures of family therapy — alongside names like Murray Bowen and Salvador Minuchin.

But Nagy took a different path from his peers. While other family therapists focused on communication patterns and power dynamics, Nagy insisted on something more fundamental: **fairness**. Not fairness as an abstract principle, but fairness as a lived, felt, intergenerational reality.

His three major works tell the story of this idea:

- **Invisible Loyalties** (1973, with Geraldine Spark) — established the foundation
- **Between Give and Take** (1986, with Barbara Krasner) — refined the theory and its clinical applications
- **Foundations of Contextual Therapy** (1987) — the comprehensive synthesis

He called his approach **Contextual Therapy** — because it insisted that you cannot understand a person without understanding the **context** of their relationships, across generations.

### [PART 2] — The Five Dimensions of Human Relationships

Nagy proposed that human relationships operate across **five dimensions** — and that most therapy only addresses two or three of them.

**Dimension 1: Facts.** The objective realities that cannot be changed — genetics, birth order, physical disability, war, poverty, adoption. You didn't choose these. They are the given conditions of your existence.

**Dimension 2: Individual Psychology.** Your inner world — defence mechanisms, cognitive patterns, emotional responses, personality. This is the terrain of psychoanalysis and cognitive-behavioural therapy.

**Dimension 3: Transactional Patterns.** The observable dynamics between people — communication styles, power structures, alliances, triangulation, scapegoating. This is where most systems-based family therapy operates.

**Dimension 4: Relational Ethics.** This is Nagy's unique contribution. The invisible ledger of fairness — who has given, who has received, who has been treated justly, and who has not. This dimension asks not "how do people communicate?" but **"is the relationship fair?"**

**Dimension 5: The Ontic Dimension.** Added later in his career — questions of existence, mortality, spiritual meaning, and intergenerational continuity. What does it mean to exist in a chain of generations? What do we owe to those who came before and those who come after?

Now, here's the crucial point for the SoE connection. Most welfare assessment systems — even sophisticated ones — operate in Dimensions 1 through 3. They assess facts, they evaluate psychological profiles, they observe behavioural patterns. But they almost never ask the Dimension 4 question: **Is the relationship between this person and the system that serves them actually fair?**

SoE's entire framework is built on asking that question.

### [PART 3] — The Ledger of Justice: Keeping the Books of Human Relationships

The heart of Nagy's theory is the **Ledger of Justice** — a metaphorical balance sheet that tracks the accumulation of **Merit** and **Debt** in human relationships.

**Merit** is earned through giving — care, attention, sacrifice, trustworthy action. When you care for your children, keep your promises, or support a struggling family member, you accumulate merit on your side of the ledger.

**Debt** is incurred through receiving — the nourishment you received as a child, the education someone paid for, the love that was invested in you. These are not shameful — they are the natural obligations of being in relationship.

In a healthy relationship, the ledger roughly balances over time. I give, you receive. You give, I receive. The balance shifts, but there is a felt sense of **reciprocity** — a sense that things are, on the whole, fair.

But when the ledger becomes severely imbalanced — when one person gives and gives and never receives, or when one person takes and takes and never acknowledges — the consequences ripple across generations.

Now, here is where the SoE connection becomes electric.

In SoE's accounting metaphor, every person has a **balance sheet** — Assets on one side, Liabilities on the other. What Nagy called "Merit" maps directly to what SoE calls "Assets" — self-esteem, skills, relationships, agency. What Nagy called "Debt" maps to what SoE calls "Liabilities" — trauma, stigma, environmental barriers.

And the critical innovation of SoE is the claim that **specific institutions keep fraudulent ledgers**. This is not an abstract "social failing." Tobashi — debt concealment — is an **active accounting fraud committed by identifiable perpetrators**: educational institutions that graduate students without delivering the developmental input they were obligated to provide, and welfare industries — particularly Japan's **Type-B Continuous Employment Support** (就労継続支援B型) facilities — that extract revenue from government contracts while transferring the structural deficit onto the individual's psychological balance sheet.

When a child is abused or neglected, specific institutions — schools, child welfare agencies, local government — incur a debt to that child. But instead of recording that debt on their books, these institutions perform a Tobashi: the liability is moved off their balance sheet and onto the individual's.

The child's suffering becomes the child's "lack of ability." The institution's unpaid obligation becomes the individual's "personal responsibility." The institution's Profit & Loss statement stays clean — graduation rates maintained, service quotas met, government subsidies collected — while the hidden liability festers in the person who was supposed to be served.

This is not a metaphor. It is **off-balance-sheet fraud** — the same structural dishonesty that brought down Enron, Olympus, and Toshiba. The only difference is that in corporate fraud, the victims are shareholders. In welfare Tobashi, the victims are the most vulnerable members of society.

Nagy would have recognised this immediately. It is exactly what he described as the pathology of the Ledger of Justice — except the perpetrators are not just dysfunctional families. They are **institutions with fiduciary obligations** that systematically breach those obligations while appearing compliant.

### [PART 4] — Invisible Loyalties: The Chains You Cannot See

One of Nagy's most powerful concepts is **Invisible Loyalties** — the hidden bonds of obligation that tie people to their families of origin, often without their awareness.

A child who was parentified — forced to become the emotional caretaker of their own parents — carries an invisible loyalty to that role even as an adult. They may become a compulsive helper, unable to say no, drawn to caregiving professions, burning out in service of others. Not because they chose this. Because the invisible ledger says: "You owe this. This is your role."

A child of an alcoholic parent may unconsciously limit their own success, because surpassing the parent feels like betrayal. The invisible loyalty whispers: "Don't outshine them. Don't leave them behind."

These loyalties are not pathological in themselves — Nagy was clear about this. Loyalty to one's family is natural and even healthy. The problem arises when loyalties become **split** — when loyalty to the past prevents a person from living fully in the present.

In the SoE framework, Invisible Loyalties help explain a phenomenon that welfare workers encounter constantly but rarely name: **why do clients sabotage their own progress?**

An employment support client who repeatedly fails job interviews, who drops out of training programmes, who cannot seem to take the final step toward independence — this person may be caught in the grip of invisible loyalties. The ledger says: "If I succeed, I abandon the world I came from. If I fail, I honour my debts to the past."

SoE's response is not to override these loyalties. It is to **make them visible** — to record them in the ledger, to give them a name, to acknowledge the debt without allowing it to imprison the person forever.

But there is a darker dimension to this story. Nagy identified a phenomenon he called **Destructive Entitlement** — the unconscious belief, born of genuine suffering, that "because I was wronged, I have the right to extract from others." In individuals, this drives the cycle of abuse. But SoE identifies the same mechanism operating at the **institutional level** — and it connects directly to what the framework calls the **Hypnos Factor**.

The Hypnos Factor — named after the Greek god of sleep — is a structural cognitive dormancy that prevents society from recognising exploitation. It is the force that tells welfare facility operators that their sub-minimum-wage workshops are "therapeutic." It is the voice that tells educational administrators that their graduation rates prove success. When institutions accumulate unchecked power over vulnerable populations, Destructive Entitlement scales: the institution, having "invested" in the client, feels entitled to extract compliance, gratitude, and above all, silence.

This is what SoE's Forced Labour Framework identifies as **Type 3: Ingratiation** — a state of psychological debt bondage where the client internalises the institution's narrative. "They're helping me. I should be grateful. I have no right to complain." The invisible loyalty is no longer to a family — it is to the institution itself.

And here is SoE's most uncomfortable extension of this insight: many support workers are themselves parentified children, now performing the same role professionally. The accumulated debt of education, medicine, and family is ultimately absorbed by individual support workers — people whose personal balance sheets are already strained. When their invisible liabilities exceed what one person can carry, the support system collapses from within. SoE asks: **Who holds the duty of care for the person providing duty of care?**

And this is precisely why the philosopher **Ingrid Robeyns'** concept of **Limitarianism** matters here. If Destructive Entitlement is the pathology, Limitarianism of Welfare Power is the structural remedy — an architectural cap on how much power any single institution can accumulate over the people it claims to serve. Input Constitutional AI is the enforcement device for that cap: it breaks the information monopoly that allows institutional Destructive Entitlement to persist unchecked.

### [PART 5] — The Revolving Slate: When Debt Passes to the Next Generation

Perhaps Nagy's most disturbing concept is the **Revolving Slate** — the mechanism by which unresolved injustice rotates from one generation to the next.

Here's how it works:

**Step 1**: A parent is harmed in childhood — abused, neglected, treated unfairly. This creates a debt on their ledger — someone owes them something that was never paid.

**Step 2**: The debt is never resolved. The parent grows up carrying what Nagy calls **Destructive Entitlement** — the unconscious belief that "because I was wronged, I have the right to collect from someone else."

**Step 3**: The "someone else" is often their own child. The parent doesn't consciously choose to harm their child. But the unresolved debt demands payment — and the child, being the most available and most vulnerable, becomes the collection point.

**Step 4**: The child now carries a new debt. And the slate revolves to the next generation.

This is not a moral failing. It is an **accounting error** — a debt charged to the wrong account, collected from the wrong person, compounding across time.

In corporate accounting, there is a term for this: **misattribution of liabilities**. A cost that belongs on one entity's books is transferred to another entity's books. The original debtor escapes accountability. The new "debtor" — who never incurred the debt — is left holding the bill.

SoE calls this exact mechanism **Tobashi** — and it identifies the perpetrators with precision. The Revolving Slate shows that Tobashi doesn't just happen within families. It operates at every level: welfare facilities that claim government subsidies while delivering sub-minimum-wage work as "training," educational institutions that issue diplomas without investing in human development, and employment agencies that place disabled workers into exploitative conditions while recording "successful outcomes."

Each of these institutions holds a **fiduciary obligation** to the people it serves. Each performs Tobashi when it records the individual's struggles as personal deficits rather than institutional failures. The information asymmetry between the institution (which controls the records) and the individual (who has no access to them) makes this fraud structurally invisible — until an auditor opens the books.

The Revolving Slate also explains why SoE integrates **Schema Therapy** into its framework. Jeffrey Young's **Early Maladaptive Schemas** — Defectiveness, Failure, Dependence — are precisely the psychological structures that form when the Revolving Slate delivers unresolved parental debt to children. These schemas are not character flaws. They are the **scar tissue** of intergenerational accounting fraud: the psychological imprint left when a child's balance sheet absorbs debts that were never theirs to carry. In SoE's Forced Labour Framework, these schemas become the mechanism by which low self-esteem becomes **fixed** — creating the vulnerability conditions that international law recognises as forced labour risk. Nagy's Revolving Slate is, in effect, the **intergenerational transmission mechanism of forced labour vulnerability**.

The solution, in both Nagy's framework and SoE's, is the same: **accurate attribution** backed by **institutional accountability**. Record the debt where it actually belongs. Name the institution that incurred it. And stop the slate from revolving by breaking the information monopoly that allows the fraud to persist.

### [PART 6] — Exoneration: "It's Not Your Fault"

If you've seen the film *Good Will Hunting*, you know the scene. Sean Maguire, played by Robin Williams, says to Will Hunting, over and over: **"It's not your fault."**

Will resists. He intellectualises. He deflects. But Sean keeps saying it, quietly, firmly, until Will's defences break and the tears come.

This scene is a near-perfect enactment of what Nagy called **Exoneration**.

Exoneration is NOT forgiveness. This is a critical distinction.

**Forgiveness** says: "I release my claim against you." But the pressure to forgive risks minimising what was done — and it places a burden on the victim to "let go" before they are ready.

**Exoneration** says: "What was done to you was real, and it was wrong. And the person who did it to you was also shaped by forces beyond their control. You are free — not because the harm didn't matter, but because carrying the debt of someone else's failures is not your obligation."

Exoneration is liberation through **contextual understanding**. It doesn't erase the past. It correctly attributes the past.

In the SoE framework, this maps directly to **Stage 1: Ledger Correction**. SoE's three stages of recovery are:

1. **Ledger Correction** (Past) — Correct the misattributed debt. The suffering that was placed on your books was never yours to carry.
2. **Self-Recognition** (Present) — You arrive at "It's not my fault" from the inside — not because someone told you, but because the corrected ledger makes it undeniable.
3. **Taking Responsibility** (Future) — "From here forward, the choices are mine." Correction of the past does not erase the future. It enables it.

This three-stage process is Exoneration made operational. And **Input Constitutional AI** — the technology at the heart of SoE — is designed to perform this function structurally: by auditing welfare records at the point of creation, ensuring that the ledger is kept honestly, and preventing the Tobashi of social debt onto individual balance sheets.

### [PART 7] — Multidirected Partiality: Taking Everyone's Side

One more concept from Nagy deserves attention, because it solves a problem that welfare systems have struggled with for decades.

In traditional therapy, the therapist is supposed to be **neutral** — not taking sides. But Nagy argued that neutrality is actually a kind of abandonment. If no one takes your side, you feel unseen. Unheard. Unrecorded.

His alternative: **Multidirected Partiality**. The therapist takes **everyone's** side — sequentially, earnestly, fully. "Tell me your perspective." "Now tell me yours." "And yours."

This is not neutrality. It is the opposite — it is radical engagement with every party's legitimate claims. And it produces something remarkable: when everyone feels heard, the system begins to self-correct.

In SoE's framework, this principle is implemented through the **double-entry bookkeeping structure** itself:

- **The client's ledger**: their strengths, their struggles, their context
- **The supporter's ledger**: their efforts, their limitations, their institutional constraints
- **The family's ledger**: their circumstances, their contributions, their own wounds
- **Society's ledger**: structural barriers, policy failures, historical debts

Nobody is made into a villain. Everyone's contribution is credited. Everyone's debt is recorded. The books are kept for all parties.

But here is the critical question: **how do you actually scale this?**

Nagy performed Multidirected Partiality through personal virtuosity — a master clinician, in the room, sequentially attending to each family member's claims. It was brilliant. It was also **completely unscalable**. It depended on one therapist's skill, in one room, with one family.

SoE's answer is a technical architecture called the **Co-LLM Session Protocol** — a three-party interaction model where a welfare professional, the client, and a Large Language Model collaborate in real time to construct the record.

Here's how it works in practice:

The professional conducts the session as they normally would. But the LLM — operating under ICAI's constitutional constraints — acts as a **structural buffer**. When the professional writes "Client shows low motivation," the LLM doesn't silently accept this. It prompts: *"Is this an individual characteristic, or is it a response to environmental factors? Has the institutional obligation been checked?"* It performs the Multidirected Partiality that Nagy demanded — not through personal charisma, but through **data structure**.

The output of this process is stored in a format called **MySupport.yaml** — a structured personal record owned by the client, not the institution. This is the technological implementation of what Nagy called "Crediting": every contribution, every struggle, every contextual factor is recorded in a format that the client can access, verify, and carry with them between services.

The key insight: Nagy's genius was **relational**. He saw what needed to happen between people. SoE's contribution is **architectural**. It takes what Nagy achieved through therapeutic artistry and encodes it into data structures (YAML), constitutional constraints (ICAI), and protocol design (Co-LLM) — so that the books are kept honestly not because the practitioner is a master clinician, but because the **system won't allow them to be kept dishonestly**.

This is what makes Input Constitutional AI fundamentally different from a simple bias-detection tool. It doesn't just flag problems — it **structurally reproduces Multidirected Partiality** at every point where a welfare record is created, with every stakeholder's perspective recorded in the same rigorous format that an auditor applies to a corporate balance sheet.

### [PART 8] — From Budapest to SoE: Why This Lineage Matters

So why does this genealogy — from Nagy to SoE — matter?

Three reasons.

**First, it grounds SoE in established theory.** The SoE accounting metaphor is not an arbitrary choice. It descends from a 50-year tradition of relational ethics that has been developed, tested, and refined in clinical practice across three continents. When SoE talks about "ledgers" and "debts" and "forced correction entries," it is speaking in a language that Nagy pioneered — and giving that language technological teeth.

**Second, it explains what AI is actually doing in this framework.** Input Constitutional AI is not a replacement for the therapeutic relationship. It is a **structural implementation of Multidirected Partiality** — a system that ensures every stakeholder's perspective is recorded, every debt is correctly attributed, and every contribution is credited. It does for institutional records what the Contextual Therapist does for family sessions: it keeps the books honest. And this matters at civilisational scale: **1.3 billion people with disabilities worldwide** are systematically excluded from databases — the largest unrecorded ledger in human history.

**Third, it reveals the structural danger that Nagy foresaw.** Nagy was a Holocaust survivor. His entire theory was born from the question: **What happens when the ledger of justice is violated on a massive scale?** His answer: the debt doesn't disappear. It revolves. It generates Destructive Entitlement — and when Destructive Entitlement is held not by an individual but by an **institution**, it becomes the Hypnos Factor: a system-wide anaesthesia that normalises exploitation while everyone sleeps.

SoE makes the same claim about the welfare system — and adds a structural prescription. The debts incurred by institutions' failure to protect vulnerable members do not disappear. They revolve — from childhood to adulthood, from the family to the welfare facility, from one generation to the next. And they will continue to revolve until **two conditions** are met: honest bookkeeping (Nagy's insight) AND an **architectural cap on institutional power** (Robeyns' Limitarianism, enforced by ICAI).

Input Constitutional AI is the technology designed to fulfil both conditions — performing continuous ledger correction at the point of data entry, while simultaneously enforcing a ceiling on the information asymmetry that allows welfare institutions to accumulate unchecked power over the people they claim to serve.

### [OUTRO] — The Ledger That Cannot Be Closed

Let me close not with an image, but with a legal fact.

**ILO Convention No. 29** — the Forced Labour Convention of 1930, ratified by 179 countries — defines forced labour as "all work or service which is exacted from any person under the menace of any penalty and for which the said person has not offered himself voluntarily."

Now consider a person with a disability enrolled in a Type-B Continuous Employment Support facility in Japan. They perform productive work. They receive a monthly "wage" averaging ¥16,507 — roughly $110 — far below minimum wage. They have limited understanding of their legal rights. Their support records — written by the institution, stored by the institution, interpreted by the institution — describe their situation as "training" and their compliance as "progress."

If the books are honest, this may indeed be legitimate training. But if the books are fraudulent — if the institution has performed a Tobashi, concealing the structural deficit and recording the person's environmental barriers as personal deficits — then the "voluntary" nature of the arrangement is compromised. The person's "consent" was manufactured by an information asymmetry they had no power to challenge.

Nagy would have recognised this as the ultimate Revolving Slate: a person carrying intergenerational debt — ACEs, educational neglect, family dysfunction — enters an institution that was supposed to correct the ledger, and instead the institution **adds new entries to the wrong side of the books**. The slate revolves one more time. The debt compounds.

And under the framework of ILO Convention 29, this is not merely an ethical failure. It is **structurally indistinguishable from forced labour**.

This is why SoE does not end with ethics. It ends with **international law**. The fire culture philosophy says: *"Questions not recorded are considered extinguished."* But the SoE framework adds a harder edge: **records that conceal exploitation are not neutral omissions. They are instruments of coercion.**

The act of keeping honest records is not just an ethical act. It is a **legal obligation**. And the failure to keep them — the Tobashi, the Revolving Slate, the institutional Destructive Entitlement that Nagy spent his life diagnosing — constitutes complicity in modern forced labour.

Ivan Boszormenyi-Nagy died in 2007. He never saw Input Constitutional AI. But the question his Ledger of Justice was designed to answer — **"Is this relationship fair?"** — has acquired, through SoE, a consequence he could not have imagined:

**If the answer is no, and the books prove it, then international law has a name for what is happening. And that name is not "service." It is "exploitation."**
