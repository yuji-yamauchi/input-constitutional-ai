# Regional meta-cognition via human perception and LLM analysis: a theoretical positioning survey

**The HOHOHO Urban Intelligence Framework proposes a genuinely novel theoretical synthesis — "urban meta-cognition" — that occupies a clear gap at the intersection of six established scholarly traditions.** No existing framework combines citizens' embodied walking experience with LLM structural analysis to produce a city's second-order self-awareness. While each individual component draws on rich intellectual lineage — from Lynch's imageability to Hutchins' distributed cognition to Clark's extended mind — the specific two-layer architecture and its meta-cognitive emergence claim have no direct precedent. This survey maps the full theoretical landscape across five research questions, identifies precise gaps, and positions HOHOHO within the existing literature.

---

## RQ1: Subjective urban experience as a form of urban knowledge

Walking-based subjective experience has been theorized as valid urban knowledge for over six decades, across at least five major intellectual traditions. **Kevin Lynch's *The Image of the City* (1960)** established that citizens' mental maps — paths, edges, districts, nodes, landmarks — constitute legitimate planning data. His concept of "imageability" positioned subjective perception as a criterion for evaluating urban form, treating citizens as active perceivers whose internal representations reveal genuine truths about city structure. Recent computational extensions by Filomena et al. (2019, *Cities*) and Ito et al. (2024) successfully extract Lynch's structural elements algorithmically, but a persistent gap remains: Lynch's underexplored "meaning" dimension — the social, emotional, and cultural significance of urban elements — has only recently been addressed by **Zhu, Brenken, Biljecki & Zhang (2026, *Environment and Planning B*)**, who use LLMs to analyze place reviews for precisely this component.

The phenomenological tradition provides deeper epistemological grounding. **Yi-Fu Tuan's "topophilia" (1974)** — the affective bond between people and place — established emotional attachment as valid geographical knowledge. **Edward Relph's "place and placelessness" (1976)** introduced the insideness/outsideness dialectic, where degrees of identification with place constitute a spectrum of experiential knowledge. **Henri Lefebvre's spatial triad (1974/1991)** offers the most comprehensive architecture: spatial practice (perceived), representations of space (conceived by planners), and representational space (lived). Lefebvre argued that conceived space systematically dominates and suppresses lived space in modern societies — a tension directly relevant to HOHOHO's premise that walking experience produces knowledge that planning orthodoxy excludes.

Walking as methodology received its most radical theoretical articulation through **Michel de Certeau's "Walking in the City" (1984)**, which distinguished the planner's panoramic view from the walker's ground-level improvisations. **Guy Debord's dérive (1955-1958)** explicitly framed subjective emotional response during walking as mapping data, while **Tim Ingold's wayfaring theory (2007, 2011)** provided the crucial distinction between wayfaring (generating knowledge through attentive movement) and transport (crossing space as mere surface). Ingold's concept of the "meshwork" — interwoven lines of movement and growth — maps precisely onto the distinction between lived urban knowledge and planned urban knowledge.

### Affect, embodiment, and the iwakan gap

**Nigel Thrift's non-representational theory (2008)** radicalizes the epistemological position by arguing that representational modes fundamentally miss the most salient characteristics of embodied existence: movement, affect, and pre-cognitive experience. **Ben Anderson's "affective atmospheres" (2009, *Emotion, Space and Society*)** — singular affective qualities emanating from but exceeding the assembling of bodies — provides vocabulary for pre-personal urban experience. **Strüver (2019, *Urban Design International*)** developed "affective urbanism" as praxis, arguing that affect falls outside representational urban design yet shapes urban experience profoundly. The **4E cognition framework** (Embodied, Embedded, Extended, Enactive) from Varela, Thompson & Rosch (1991) and Gallagher (2017) provides the cognitive science foundation for treating bodily urban experience as genuine cognition rather than mere sensation.

A critical discovery: **the Japanese concept of iwakan (違和感) has no established presence in urban studies literature.** Iwakan — literally "a sense of disharmony" — describes a subtle but unambiguous bodily feeling that something does not fit. Linguistically and phenomenologically, it occupies a space between Tuan's topophilia (positive attachment), Relph's outsideness (diminished identification), Anderson's atmospheric affect (pre-personal intensity), and the Situationists' psychic ambiance (emotional zones). Iwakan is distinct from all of these: it is **somatic urban intelligence** — the body registering urban dysfunction before the mind articulates it. The closest theoretical parallel is Derrida's "trace" — as explored in a *Kinephanos* article (2015) connecting iwakan to moments when surfaces crack open to reveal underlying structures. The *IWAKAN Magazine* (2020-2023) demonstrates the concept's cultural currency as an epistemological tool of productive discomfort. This represents a significant theoretical innovation opportunity for the HOHOHO framework.

**Key gap identified**: While multiple traditions treat subjective experience as valid urban knowledge, no existing framework theorizes the aggregation of individual subjective urban experiences into collective urban cognition — a city's self-awareness of its own condition through its citizens' bodies.

## RQ2: Theoretical foundations of human-as-sensor and participatory sensing

The trajectory from technical sensing to human-centered urban knowledge production spans two decades and reflects a fundamental epistemological shift. **Burke & Estrin's participatory sensing (2006, UCLA CENS)** established that mobile devices enable citizens to "gather, analyze and share local knowledge," making the crucial claim that spatiotemporal context validates citizen-collected data more than the gatherer's identity. **Michael Goodchild's "Citizens as Sensors" (2007, *GeoJournal*)** coined Volunteered Geographic Information (VGI) and theorized a special epistemic status for place-based lay knowledge, distinguishing it from expert geographic knowledge while arguing for their complementarity.

The critical turn came with **Jennifer Gabrys's "Programming Environments" (2014, *Environment and Planning D*)**, which reframed citizen sensing through Foucault's "environmentality." Gabrys argues that citizen sensing is never neutral knowledge production but a political-material practice that reconfigures who counts as a citizen and what counts as environmental knowledge. The sensing citizen becomes "an expression of productive infrastructures" — citizenship articulated environmentally through data practices. Her ERC-funded Citizen Sense project (2013-2018, Cambridge/Goldsmiths) revealed the tension between empowerment (democratizing monitoring) and discipline (responsibilizing citizens as data nodes). This critical perspective is essential for the HOHOHO framework to address: **who controls the LLM, whose walking experiences count, and how biases in AI interpretation are managed**.

The **Urban Emotions framework (Resch & Zeile, 2013-2016)** bridges psychogeography and digital sensing by combining calibrated biosensors (GSR, heart rate), subjective citizen observations, and crowdsourced social media data. Resch's "People as Sensors" concept (2013, Springer) disambiguates three overlapping paradigms: people as sensors (subjective observations), collective sensing (aggregated anonymous data), and citizen science (elevating local expertise). The framework draws on Lynch and Debord explicitly, connecting contemporary biosensing to historical traditions of subjective urban perception. Their core claim — that "a city requires Smart Citizens to be intelligent itself" — prefigures the HOHOHO framework's argument.

**Christian Nold's Emotional Cartography (2009)** is perhaps the closest precursor to HOHOHO's concept of human perception as the first cognitive layer. Through biomapping workshops involving **2,000+ participants across 25+ cities**, Nold combined GSR measurement with GPS to record physiological arousal alongside location. Critically, the biometric data functioned not as objective truth but as a **boundary object** enabling collective interpretation — knowledge emerged through group discussion, not direct measurement. This constructivist-participatory epistemology, referencing Foucault's "technologies of the self," positions sensing as knowledge-constitutive rather than knowledge-reflecting.

Recent developments in citizen science epistemology provide formal grounding. **Jaeger et al. (2023, *Royal Society Open Science*)** proposed three philosophical pillars: perspectival realism (multiple valid perspectives), naturalistic process-based epistemology (quality assessed through process), and deliberative social practices (knowledge through argument). **Haklay's four-level hierarchy (2013)** ranges from crowdsourcing (citizens as data collectors) through distributed intelligence and participatory science to **extreme citizen science** (citizens as full collaborators). The HOHOHO framework operates between levels 1 and 3, with its LLM layer introducing a novel analytical intermediary.

**Critical gap**: No existing framework provides a unified epistemology bridging from subjective individual perception → collective urban knowledge → actionable intelligence, and none anticipates LLM-mediated analysis as an interpretive intermediary. The HOHOHO framework would be the first to propose AI-mediated citizen sensing as a systematic theoretical architecture.

## RQ3: LLMs as cognitive partners in urban and narrative analysis

The application of LLMs to urban research has exploded since 2023, yet the field's theoretical maturity lags far behind its technical capabilities. **Corchado & Fu (2025, *Journal of Urban Technology*)** reviewed 58 articles and developed a conceptual framework mapping LLM capabilities to urban planning tasks, identifying a shift from "passive tool-assisted workflows to active human-AI collaborative partnerships." **Zheng, Xu, Lin, Santi, Ratti, Wang & Li (2025, *Nature Computational Science*)** proposed LLMs as "intelligent assistants" through three stages: conceptualization, generation, and evaluation. A parallel survey (2025, ScienceDirect) explicitly frames the transition as "From Tools to Partners," establishing a taxonomy of collaboration patterns — the closest existing framing to HOHOHO's "cognitive partner" concept, though it lacks a theoretical mechanism for how partnership generates emergent knowledge.

Specific urban LLM systems now include **UrbanGPT (2024, ACM SIGKDD)** for spatio-temporal prediction, **CityGPT (2025, SIGKDD)** for urban spatial cognition, and **multi-agent LLM frameworks for participatory planning** that simulate thousands of citizen profiles providing feedback (Zhou, Lin, Jin & Li, 2024). The participatory planning work is particularly relevant: it uses synthetic citizens rather than real narratives, highlighting precisely the gap HOHOHO addresses by using actual human walking experiences.

### Theoretical frameworks for AI as cognitive partner

Several philosophical frameworks have emerged for theorizing LLM epistemic roles. The **Knowledge-Building Partner (KBP) concept (2025, MDPI AI)** builds on Peirce's theory of inquiry and Stanovich's tripartite cognition model to propose "an AI system designed to support inquiry without claiming epistemic authority," identifying a principled division of epistemic labor where LLMs contribute pattern recognition while humans maintain reflective control. A paper in *AI & Society* (2025) develops collective epistemology theory, arguing LLMs approximate "reliabilist justification" but not "reflective justification." The concept of **"Epistemia"** (arXiv, 2025) names the condition where "linguistic plausibility substitutes for epistemic evaluation" — identifying seven epistemic fault lines between human and AI judgment.

The **sympoietic approach** (2025, *Discourse*, Taylor & Francis) draws on Donna Haraway's "making-with" to position generative AI within an ecosystem of co-composition, offering a theoretical frame closest to HOHOHO's meta-cognition concept. **Andy Clark's "Extending Minds with Generative AI" (2025, *Nature Communications*)** directly addresses LLMs as cognitive extensions, describing "bio-technological hybrid minds" and calling for new epistemological frameworks. The **"System 0" proposal** (arXiv, 2025) positions AI as a cognitive layer preceding both System 1 (intuitive) and System 2 (deliberative) thinking.

### The walkability perception gap

A key empirical finding: a 2025 *PLOS ONE* study directly comparing **GPT-4o and human perceptions of walkability** found that humans focus on "green," "grass," "quiet," "lively" (sensory-emotional qualities), while GPT-4o emphasizes "urban," "area," "street," "mobility" (functional attributes). This identifies the exact complementarity that HOHOHO exploits: human perception captures experiential qualities that AI misses, while AI can structurally analyze patterns across many human accounts that no individual perceiver could synthesize alone.

**Critical counter-perspective**: **Messeri & Crockett (2024, *Nature*, 373+ citations)** warn against "illusions of understanding" when AI is treated as a "trusted partner," identifying risks of "monocultures of knowing." The HOHOHO framework must explicitly engage this critique by demonstrating that its two-layer architecture produces genuine understanding rather than its appearance.

**Assessment of theoretical maturity**: Applications of LLMs in urban contexts are mature; LLMs for qualitative analysis are emerging; LLMs for urban citizen voice are nascent; **LLM analysis as producing "second-order" or "meta-level" urban understanding is virtually absent**. The "third-order knowledge" concept (*AI & Society*, 2026) is the closest but does not apply to urban analysis.

## RQ4: Collective meta-cognition — the theoretical core

This is the most critical theoretical question for the HOHOHO framework, and the findings reveal both existing foundations and a genuine conceptual gap. **The concept of meta-cognition at collective and organizational levels does exist**, though it operates under various names that "paradoxically do not recognize one another" (Kellogg School review, Northwestern).

**Organizational meta-cognition** is well-established: Argyris & Schön's (1978) "deutero-learning" describes organizations "learning how to carry out single-loop and double-loop learning." The concept is defined as "knowing what an organization knows" — including, crucially, awareness of ignorance as a motivation for learning. **Michael West's team reflexivity (1996)** — "the extent to which group members overtly reflect upon the group's objectives, strategies, and processes" — is arguably the closest existing concept to urban meta-cognition at the collective level. **Frith (2012, *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*)** argued that explicit metacognition evolved through its enhancement of collaborative decision-making — metacognition inherently serves a social function.

**Edwin Hutchins' distributed cognition (*Cognition in the Wild*, 1995)** establishes that "cultural activity systems have cognitive properties of their own that are different from the cognitive properties of the individuals who participate in them." The navigation team on a ship performs cognition as a system. However, Hutchins focuses on first-order cognitive processes — computation, memory, problem-solving — and **does not explicitly theorize reflexive/meta-cognitive properties at the system level**. This is the first key gap.

### Lévy's reflexive collective intelligence — the closest precedent

**Pierre Lévy's work on "reflexive collective intelligence" (2010, *Information Sciences*)** is the most directly relevant existing theoretical construct. Through his IEML research program, Lévy explicitly aimed to "allow collective intelligence to be reflexive" — enabling collective intelligence to observe and understand itself. His vision transcends "wisdom of the crowd" by assuming dialogue and reflexivity. However, Lévy's reflexivity is mediated through semantic computing in digital/cyber space rather than citizen-AI perception in urban physical space.

**Second-order cybernetics** provides the deepest theoretical resonance. **Heinz von Foerster (1974)** defined second-order cybernetics as "the cybernetics of observing systems" — systems that observe themselves. **Niklas Luhmann** applied autopoiesis to social systems, treating organizations as self-observing, self-reproducing communication systems. The HOHOHO framework's concept of a city having "second-order awareness of its own condition" maps directly onto this tradition. Yet **no one has explicitly combined second-order cybernetics with urban sensing to theorize the city as a meta-cognitive system**.

The **extended mind thesis (Clark & Chalmers, 1998)** and its social extensions (Islam, 2014, *Human Relations*) provide philosophical foundations for treating city-scale sociotechnical systems as cognitive systems, though they primarily address first-order cognition. **Goldman's social epistemology (1999, 2010)** evaluates how social systems produce knowledge, with his "systems-oriented" approach asking how to design systems whose goals include epistemic goods. **Cook & Karvonen (2024, *Urban Studies*)** apply Aristotelian epistemology to smart cities, distinguishing techne, episteme, and phronesis — arguing that big data urbanism represents "a new mode of urban knowledge politics" that may produce "impoverished modes of urban development" without complementary knowledge forms.

The **EU Collective Awareness Platforms (CAPS, 2013-2017)** theorize collective awareness as distributed between humans and technologies — a precursor to urban meta-cognition — but focus on awareness of external problems rather than a city's self-awareness. "Cognitive city" literature conceptualizes cities as learning systems with an "intelligence amplification loop" where humans and computers learn from each other, but **lacks the reflexive/self-awareness dimension** that distinguishes meta-cognition from cognition.

### Gap analysis for "urban meta-cognition"

| Tradition | Contribution | Gap filled by HOHOHO |
|---|---|---|
| Team reflexivity (West, 1996) | Group-level self-awareness | Not scaled to cities; no AI layer |
| Organizational metacognition | "Knowing what the org knows" | Limited to bounded organizations |
| Distributed cognition (Hutchins) | System-level cognition | No meta-cognitive dimension |
| Reflexive CI (Lévy, 2010) | Self-aware collective intelligence | Digital/semantic, not urban/perceptual |
| Second-order cybernetics | Self-observing systems | Not applied to citizen-AI urban systems |
| Social sensing (Wang et al.) | Citizens as sensors | Technical, not framed as metacognition |
| Cognitive cities | City as learning system | No reflexive self-awareness component |

**Assessment: "Urban meta-cognition" is a genuine theoretical synthesis.** It is best presented as: the application of second-order cybernetic principles to the sociotechnical urban system, operationalized through the synthesis of social sensing (citizen perception) and computational analysis (AI), extending organizational metacognition and team reflexivity to the city scale, and providing the reflexive dimension that cognitive city frameworks currently lack.

## RQ5: The two-layer model as a novel research framework

The human-AI hybrid intelligence literature provides substantial foundations but leaves a specific space that HOHOHO occupies. **Ece Kamar's hybrid intelligence framework (2016, IJCAI)** established human-AI systems as the unit of analysis. The **COHUMAIN framework (Gupta & Woolley, 2025, *Topics in Cognitive Science*)** — Collective Human-Machine Intelligence — is the most theoretically developed model for emergence, using a Transactive Systems Model with three interlocking sociocognitive systems: collective memory, attention, and reasoning. **Yasseri et al. (2024, *Patterns*/Cell Press)** propose a multilayer network representation of human-AI collective intelligence, claiming "combining strengths can create a level of collective intelligence greater than the sum of its parts."

In urban contexts specifically, **Yue et al. (2025, *Computational Urban Science*)** propose "Human-AI Symbiosis" for sustainable cities — the closest existing urban framework. They argue urban informatics grapples with "human-centric complexity encompassing subjective perceptions, socio-political dynamics, and multifaceted challenges that defy deterministic solutions." However, this framework is programmatic and high-level, lacking HOHOHO's specific cognitive architecture (perception layer → AI analysis layer → emergent meta-cognition). **Luo et al. (2025, *Cities*)** developed perception-powered urban digital twins combining visual perception via VR with ML prediction — close in spirit but focused on visual features rather than narrative/emotional experience, and using random forest ML rather than LLM interpretation.

A meta-analytic review (arXiv, 2025) spanning 60 years from Licklider's "man-computer symbiosis" to contemporary models reveals a crucial "performance paradox": human-AI teams show **negative synergy in judgment/decision tasks** but **positive synergy in content creation and problem formulation**. HOHOHO should position itself explicitly in the latter domain — its contribution is problem formulation (identifying what is wrong with urban infrastructure) and content creation (generating urban narratives and structural knowledge), not judgment optimization.

**Andy Clark's "Extending Minds with Generative AI" (2025, *Nature Communications*)** is a landmark paper directly extending the Extended Mind thesis to LLMs, describing "bio-technological hybrid minds" and calling for "extended cognitive hygiene." The "System 0" proposal (arXiv, 2025) positions AI as a cognitive extension preceding both intuitive and deliberative thinking. Neither addresses urban contexts or collective-scale cognition, but both provide philosophical scaffolding for HOHOHO's claim that LLM analysis constitutes a cognitive layer.

### What makes HOHOHO genuinely novel

The specific combination of ALL the following has **no direct precedent** in the literature:

- **Layer 1** = Embodied subjective walking experience (emotions, narratives, photos, iwakan) — not biosensor data, not VR, but naturalistic phenomenological experience
- **Layer 2** = LLM structural analysis of qualitative human data — not traditional ML or CNN, but large language model interpretation
- **Emergence claim** = "Urban meta-cognition" — the city gaining second-order awareness through this combination
- **Cultural-philosophical concept** (iwakan) as systematic urban data input

Each component has precedents; the synthesis is novel. Novelty is assessed as **moderate-to-high**.

## Theoretical framework: positioning HOHOHO

The HOHOHO framework should be positioned as building on and synthesizing six intellectual traditions:

1. **Phenomenological urbanism** (Tuan, Relph, Lefebvre) → legitimizes subjective experience as urban knowledge
2. **Participatory sensing theory** (Burke/Estrin, Goodchild, Resch/Zeile, Gabrys) → establishes human-as-sensor paradigm
3. **Distributed/collective cognition** (Hutchins, Woolley, Lévy) → proves systems can have cognitive properties beyond individual members
4. **Second-order cybernetics** (von Foerster, Luhmann) → provides the theoretical template for self-observing systems
5. **Extended mind + AI** (Clark 2025, "System 0") → grounds AI as cognitive extension
6. **LLM urban applications** (Corchado & Fu 2025, Zheng et al. 2025) → demonstrates technical feasibility

The recommended theoretical formulation:

> **Urban meta-cognition** is the application of second-order cybernetic principles to the sociotechnical urban system, operationalized through a two-layer architecture where citizens' embodied perceptions (including iwakan — somatic sensing of urban disharmony) constitute the first cognitive layer, and LLM structural analysis of those perceptions constitutes the second cognitive layer. Together, they produce the city's reflexive awareness of its own infrastructure and livability — what organizational theorists call "knowing what we know" (organizational metacognition), extended to the urban scale through human-AI cognitive partnership.

## Gap analysis: what has NOT been theorized

Five specific gaps constitute the HOHOHO framework's contribution space:

1. **No unified framework aggregates subjective walking experiences into collective urban cognition.** Multiple traditions validate individual subjective experience, but none proposes a mechanism for structural analysis of aggregated experiences into second-order awareness.

2. **Lynch's "meaning" dimension remains computationally underexplored.** Until Zhu et al. (2026), the social-emotional-cultural significance of urban elements had been systematically neglected in computational urbanism.

3. **Iwakan has no established urban theory.** This Japanese phenomenological concept fills a gap between topophilia, outsideness, affective atmospheres, and psychogeographic ambiance — offering a vocabulary for somatic urban intelligence that Western theories lack.

4. **Walking-based knowledge lacks structural analysis infrastructure.** Walking as method is well-established; systematic AI processing of walking-based subjective data is not.

5. **No framework combines second-order cybernetics with urban sensing through human-AI architecture.** Each tradition exists independently; the synthesis is genuinely novel.

## Research positioning: where HOHOHO sits

HOHOHO does not fit neatly into a single disciplinary home. It sits at a crossroads:

- **Urban Computing / Urban Informatics**: The computational infrastructure and data processing dimension. Closest established field, but typically lacks phenomenological depth.
- **Social Epistemology**: The knowledge-production claims. HOHOHO proposes a new mode of urban knowledge generation, making it an epistemological intervention.
- **Human-AI Collaborative Intelligence**: The cognitive architecture. COHUMAIN (Gupta & Woolley) provides the closest theoretical home for the emergence claim.
- **Affective Urbanism / Environmental Psychology**: The human perception layer. Connects to Thrift, Anderson, and the emotional geographies tradition.
- **Second-Order Cybernetics / Systems Theory**: The meta-cognitive framing. Von Foerster and Luhmann provide the deepest theoretical resonance.

**Recommended primary positioning**: Urban Intelligence / Computational Urban Science, with explicit theoretical grounding in second-order cybernetics and social epistemology. The framework should be presented as bridging Lefebvre's "lived space" (which resists systematization) and "conceived space" (which systematizes but suppresses lived experience) through a new AI-mediated mode of urban knowledge production.

## Conclusion: a framework positioned in genuine theoretical white space

This survey reveals that the HOHOHO framework's "urban meta-cognition" concept is not a mere rebranding of existing ideas but occupies a specific and defensible theoretical gap. The concept has clear intellectual ancestors — West's team reflexivity, Hutchins' distributed cognition, Lévy's reflexive collective intelligence, von Foerster's second-order cybernetics, Resch's urban emotions, Clark's extended mind — but **no existing work synthesizes embodied citizen perception, LLM structural analysis, and meta-cognitive emergence at the urban scale**. The introduction of iwakan as a phenomenological category for somatic urban sensing adds a culturally grounded conceptual innovation with no precedent in the English-language urban studies literature.

Three theoretical moves distinguish HOHOHO: first, scaling organizational meta-cognition ("knowing what we know") to the city; second, operationalizing this through a specific two-layer human-AI architecture rather than abstract programmatic claims; and third, grounding the human perception layer in phenomenological walking experience rather than biosensor data or social media mining. The framework should explicitly engage Messeri & Crockett's (2024) "illusions of understanding" critique and the empirical finding of negative synergy in human-AI judgment tasks, positioning its contribution in the domain of problem formulation and knowledge emergence where positive synergy is demonstrated. The resulting theoretical contribution — that a city can develop reflexive self-awareness of its own livability through the combination of citizens' embodied discomfort and AI structural analysis — is both intellectually ambitious and, given the mapped theoretical landscape, genuinely novel.
