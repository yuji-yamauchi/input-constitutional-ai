Dear Susan,

Thank you for your prompt response and the invaluable materials you shared.

I have carefully read both attached papers and your four-category barrier framework. What struck me most was how the same challenge appears completely different depending on one's standpoint: employers ask "why won't they make rational choices?", job seekers ask "why are there so many barriers?", and policymakers ask "why aren't we seeing results?" Each sees a different reality, yet the underlying challenge—how to reconcile what appears as "irrational choice"—remains common across all perspectives.

I recently met with Professor Niekawa, my prospective supervisor at Japan University of Social Welfare. We shared this same concern. Moreover, he recognizes the resistance to change within the existing welfare sector as an even greater challenge than I had anticipated.

The question you raised—"why does this person expect special treatment?"—is precisely what SoE attempts to answer. My hypothesis is that this "expectation" itself is formed as an unintended consequence of well-meaning support and institutional design—an environmental factor, not a personal trait. Using the accounting metaphor, we can re-categorize what appears to be an "attitude problem" as a "system design problem."

I realized the core issue may be a wrong assessment formula: many systems start with a fixed service menu and reverse-engineer "deficits" in the person to justify fitting them into that menu. In SoE, I flip this: fix the goal as equal-basis employment participation, then treat barriers as accumulated environmental liabilities (employer/policy/society) that must be identified and settled. Your barrier classification maps naturally onto a chart of accounts for these liabilities, and your point about measuring staff competencies/performance becomes a concrete output of the model.

I have one request. If I am admitted to the graduate program, I would like to ask for an interview as part of my master's research regarding the friction and resistance that occurred during the UK's transition from Sheltered Employment to Supported Employment. Japan is currently in the midst of this very transition, and your experience and insights would be invaluable.

I will contact you again once I receive my application results.

Best regards,

Yuji Yamauchi
