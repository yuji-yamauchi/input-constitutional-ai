Dear Susan,

Thank you for your prompt response and the invaluable materials you shared.

I have carefully read both attached papers—including the Job Market Re-design framework and your four-category barrier framework. Your work on "Disability Confident" employers and the Three Talents Bridge Academies model particularly resonated with me. The results—94% of entrants graduating, 91% gaining formal certification, and 96% of those certified securing paid internships or jobs—demonstrate what becomes possible when the focus shifts to making it easier for employers, which in turn makes it easier for job seekers with disabilities.

What struck me most was how the same challenge appears completely different depending on one's standpoint: employers ask "why won't they make rational choices?", job seekers ask "why are there so many barriers?", and policymakers ask "why aren't we seeing results?" Each sees a different reality, yet the underlying challenge—how to reconcile what appears as "irrational choice"—remains common across all perspectives.

Interestingly, Japan introduced an "Employment Passport" (就労パスポート) system approximately five years ago, which shares some conceptual DNA with your frameworks. I suspect Japanese policymakers may have encountered your materials through international channels. However, when I met with the Ministry official responsible for this initiative before its launch, I learned that an advisory committee had insisted the passport must create "no advantage for having it, and no disadvantage for not having it." It was reduced to a mere recommended format—a document with no consequence. The official's resigned expression said everything words could not. Japan's employment support system is perhaps 30 years behind the UK's current state, and this episode illustrates why.

I recently met with Professor Niekawa, my prospective supervisor at Japan University of Social Welfare. We shared this same concern. Moreover, he recognizes the resistance to change within the existing welfare sector as an even greater challenge than I had anticipated.

The question you raised—"why does this person expect special treatment?"—is precisely what SoE attempts to answer. My hypothesis is that this "expectation" itself is formed as an unintended consequence of well-meaning support and institutional design—an environmental factor, not a personal trait. Using the accounting metaphor, we can re-categorize what appears to be an "attitude problem" as a "system design problem."

I realized the core issue may be a wrong assessment formula: many systems start with a fixed service menu and reverse-engineer "deficits" in the person to justify fitting them into that menu. In SoE, I flip this: fix the goal as equal-basis employment participation, then treat barriers as accumulated environmental liabilities (employer/policy/society) that must be identified and settled. Your barrier classification maps naturally onto a chart of accounts for these liabilities, and your point about measuring staff competencies/performance becomes a concrete output of the model.

This 30-year gap, however, may present an unexpected opportunity. Just as many African countries leapfrogged landline infrastructure entirely and built mobile networks from scratch, Japan might reconstruct its employment support system based on current resources—scientific assessment tools, IT platforms, and frameworks like yours—rather than incrementally patching an outdated system. SoE is designed with this leapfrog potential in mind.

I have one request. If I am admitted to the graduate program, I would like to ask for an interview as part of my master's research regarding the friction and resistance that occurred during the UK's transition from Sheltered Employment to Supported Employment. Japan is currently in the midst of this very transition, and your experience and insights would be invaluable.

I will contact you again once I receive my application results.

Best regards,

Yuji Yamauchi
