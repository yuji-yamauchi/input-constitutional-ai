# Paternalism in disability welfare through a cognitive science lens

**Paternalism in disability welfare services is best understood not as a moral failing but as a predictable outcome of cognitive architecture.** Support workers operating under cognitive load default to System 1 heuristic processing, activating disability stereotypes and loss-averse risk calculations that systematically favor restriction over autonomy. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: professional cognitive shortcuts produce paternalistic decisions, which induce learned helplessness in service users, whose reduced initiative then confirms professionals' assumptions about incapacity. Breaking this cycle requires interventions targeting both the cognitive mechanisms driving professional behavior and the institutional choice architectures that make paternalism the path of least resistance.

The evidence base spans behavioral economics (Kahneman, Thaler & Sunstein), attribution theory (Abramson, Seligman & Teasdale), self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan), and disability rights law (CRPD Article 12). In the Japanese context, the 2017 MHLW Decision-Making Support Guidelines and the quasi-mandate contract (準委任契約) framework provide distinctive anti-paternalism tools, though the CRPD Committee's 2022 Concluding Observations found Japan's progress insufficient. Empirical studies measuring paternalistic attitudes reveal a striking pattern: professionals consistently report egalitarian explicit attitudes while harboring moderate-to-strong implicit bias favoring nondisabled people — a phenomenon termed "aversive ableism."

## Philosophical taxonomy: Four species of paternalism in disability services

Gerald Dworkin's classic formulation defines paternalism as interference with a person's liberty or autonomy, without their consent, justified by the belief that this promotes their welfare (Dworkin, 1972, *The Monist*; updated 2020, *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy*). In disability welfare, this interference assumes distinct forms that must be differentiated for analysis.

**Hard paternalism** permits overriding a person's decisions even when they act with full knowledge and voluntariness. This underpins traditional guardianship systems and involuntary institutionalization. Joel Feinberg's contrasting **soft paternalism** (*Harm to Self*, 1986) justifies intervention only to verify whether conduct is substantially voluntary and informed — once voluntariness is confirmed, the person must be free to proceed. The hard/soft distinction maps directly onto the disability services debate: soft paternalism aligns with supported decision-making (intervening to ensure understanding), while hard paternalism underpins substituted decision-making (overriding expressed preferences). Douglas and Bigby (2021, *Disability & Society*) observed that even in ostensibly supported decision-making practice, supporters apply a "social realism test" about achievability that can constitute covert hard paternalism.

**Libertarian paternalism**, coined by Thaler and Sunstein (2003, *American Economic Review*; elaborated in *Nudge*, 2008), steers choices through "choice architecture" — manipulating defaults, framing, and starting points — while preserving freedom to opt out. This raises unique concerns in disability contexts because default options disproportionately affect persons with cognitive disabilities who have difficulty recognizing and exercising opt-out mechanisms. Shane Ryan (2018, *Analysis*) argued that libertarian paternalism is functionally hard paternalism.

**Epistemic paternalism**, defined by Alvin Goldman (1991, *The Journal of Philosophy*) as interposing one's judgment over another's regarding their epistemic welfare, is particularly salient in disability services where professionals control information flows — filtering, simplifying, or withholding information about rights, options, and risks. The justification that the person "cannot adequately process" full information directly invokes the cognitive capacity judgments that the CRPD Committee challenges. Ahlstrom-Vij (2013, *Epistemic Paternalism: A Defence*) and Bernal & Axtell (2020) have elaborated the concept's justificatory conditions, which require meeting interference, non-consultation, and improvement conditions simultaneously.

Beyond these four types, Lawrence Mead's "new paternalism" (1997, Brookings) — directive and supervisory welfare policies — manifests through conditional benefit programs. Andrew Batavia (2001, *Journal of Disability Policy Studies*) applied this specifically to disability policy. In practice, paternalism in disability services manifests through six interrelated mechanisms: formal guardianship, service system control over goals and activities, information gatekeeping, risk-averse organizational cultures, informal family decision-making that supplants the person's will, and "best interests" clinical overrides.

## The cognitive bias cascade driving professional paternalism

Cognitive biases in disability services do not operate in isolation. They form a **cascading sequence**: a diagnostic label anchors initial expectations, the representativeness heuristic matches the individual to a disability prototype, confirmation bias selectively filters subsequent information, diagnostic overshadowing attributes new symptoms to the disability, the availability heuristic makes negative outcomes cognitively salient, status quo bias perpetuates restrictive arrangements, and implicit bias provides the unconscious substrate on which all these mechanisms operate.

**Diagnostic overshadowing** is the most extensively documented bias specific to disability services. Reiss, Levitan, and Szyszko (1982, *American Journal of Mental Deficiency*) coined the term to describe how intellectual disability diagnoses are so salient that they "overshadow" co-occurring mental health conditions, whose symptoms are falsely attributed to the disability itself. Critically, Reiss and Szyszko (1983) found this bias was **unrelated to professional experience** — years of practice offered no protection. The most recent systematic review by Dell'Armo and Tassé (2024, *American Journal on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities*) evaluated all 25 studies to date, finding the phenomenon "may not be as ubiquitous as originally believed" but grading the overall evidence quality as "Low" due to reliance on vignette methodologies. Mason and Scior (2004, *Journal of Applied Research in Intellectual Disabilities*), studying 133 UK psychologists and 90 psychiatrists, found clinicians were more likely to recognize a range of symptoms in persons with IQs in the normal range than those with intellectual disabilities.

The cognitive mechanisms underlying diagnostic overshadowing combine **anchoring** (IQ scores and diagnostic labels as numerical/categorical anchors from which professionals insufficiently adjust), the **representativeness heuristic** (matching symptoms to the "prototype" of disability behavior rather than considering base rates of psychiatric disorders), the **availability heuristic** (the disability diagnosis being the most cognitively accessible explanation), and **confirmation bias** (selectively attending to symptoms consistent with disability while overlooking psychiatric symptoms). Harding (2004, *Clinical Psychology Review*) directly applied the Tversky-Kahneman heuristics framework to disability determinations, arguing these heuristics introduce "considerable error." A major scoping review by Featherston, Downie, Vogel, and Galvin (2020, *PLoS ONE*) identified **149 studies** examining biases in allied health professionals, finding that **77% of studies reported at least one outcome representing the presence of bias**.

**Implicit bias toward people with disabilities** has been documented at scale. VanPuymbrouck, Friedman, and Feldner (2020, *Rehabilitation Psychology*) analyzed data from **25,006 healthcare providers** via Project Implicit, finding that while explicit attitudes indicated little prejudice (M = 4.41), implicit attitudes revealed **moderate preference for nondisabled people** (M = 0.54). The majority were classified as "aversive ableists" — low explicit prejudice coexisting with high implicit prejudice. A 2024 analysis of **24,545 nurses** found an even more striking pattern: nurses had more positive explicit attitudes toward people with disabilities than other groups but simultaneously **more negative implicit attitudes**. Wilson and Scior's (2014, *Research in Developmental Disabilities*) systematic review of 17 IAT studies confirmed "a consistent pattern of moderate to strong negative implicit attitudes towards individuals with disabilities" with "little to no association between explicit and implicit attitudes." Friedman (2025, *Social Work*) found that among **5,167 social service professionals**, 70.3% reported no explicit preference, but **77.8% showed implicit preference for people without disabilities**.

The **status quo bias** (Samuelson & Zeckhauser, 1988, *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*) operates through loss aversion, sunk cost thinking, and regret avoidance. In disability services, existing support arrangements become self-perpetuating defaults — maintaining restrictions requires no action, while pursuing greater autonomy involves perceived risk. A 2022 paper directly applied status quo bias to learning disability services, conceptualizing practitioner "inertia" as "unwillingness to abandon the status quo irrespective of alternatives." This intersects with **omission bias** — professionals preferring harms caused by inaction (maintained restrictions) over equivalent harms caused by action (increased autonomy with some risk).

## Dual-process theory and prospect theory explain systematic risk aversion

Kahneman's dual-process framework provides the overarching cognitive architecture explanation. **System 1** (fast, automatic, heuristic-driven) processing dominates when support workers face cognitive busyness, time pressure, positive mood, or emotional fatigue — conditions endemic in social care settings. Under System 1 dominance, disability stereotypes activate automatically, risk assessments default to the most cognitively available negative outcome, and decisions satisfice toward familiar, restrictive arrangements. **System 2** (slow, deliberative, analytical) processing is most needed for person-centered planning and risk enablement — precisely the situations where workload pressures make it least available.

Saltiel (2016, *British Journal of Social Work*) studied front-line decision-making in child protection and found that social workers "do not rely entirely on guidance on the threshold criterion... Instead, they use discretionary intuition and analytical judgment," defaulting to heuristic processing when guidance is inadequate. While no empirical studies directly apply dual-process theory to disability support worker decisions (a significant literature gap), the theoretical application is robust.

**Prospect theory** (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979, *Econometrica*) explains why support workers systematically overweight negative outcomes. Loss aversion — losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains — means the potential costs of a failed autonomy expansion (injury, complaint, regulatory sanction) psychologically dominate the potential benefits (increased independence, self-esteem, skill development). **Reference dependence** compounds this: if the reference point is the person's current restricted independence level, any move toward greater autonomy is framed as a "gamble" rather than as restoring rightful autonomy. The **certainty effect** leads professionals to overvalue the "certain" safety of restriction relative to the "probable" benefit of greater freedom.

**Cognitive load** provides the physiological mechanism. Pignatiello, Martin, and Hickman (2020, *Journal of Health Psychology*) defined decision fatigue as progressive deterioration in decision-making quality following extended decision-making periods. Under cognitive load, individuals become more susceptible to heuristic use and "psychological myopia" — focusing on immediately salient information while ignoring background factors. Gilbert, Pelham, and Krull (1988) demonstrated that under cognitive load, people's ability to use situational cues deteriorates — overloaded support workers are less likely to consider individual circumstances and more likely to rely on diagnostic stereotypes. High caseloads, emotional demands, and time pressure create conditions where paternalistic "safe" decisions become cognitively efficient defaults.

The service user side of this equation is explained by **learned helplessness**. Critically, Maier and Seligman's (2016, *Annual Review of Psychology*) neuroscience-informed revision of the original theory established that **passivity is the default, unlearned response to prolonged aversive events** — what must be actively learned is control and agency. This reversal has profound implications: if service environments do not actively teach and support control, helplessness persists as the default state. The attributional reformulation by Abramson, Seligman, and Teasdale (1978, *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*) identified three dimensions — stability, globality, and internality — through which helplessness becomes entrenched. Paternalistic service delivery creates a "perfect storm": internal attributions ("I cannot do this because of who I am"), stable attributions ("this will always be true"), and global attributions ("I am helpless across all domains"). Smoleń and Pilecka (2017, *Advances in Cognitive Psychology*) directly tested learned helplessness in people with intellectual disability, finding impaired escape/avoidance learning that persisted after one week.

**Self-determination theory** (Ryan & Deci, 2000, *American Psychologist*) identifies three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — whose thwarting produces diminished motivation and well-being. Frielink, Schuengel, and Embregts (2018, *American Journal on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities*) tested SDT with 186 adults with intellectual disability, finding that perceived autonomy support was positively associated with autonomous motivation, need satisfaction, and psychological well-being. The SDT autonomy-control continuum maps directly onto the cognitive mechanisms of learned helplessness: external regulation (others controlling behavior) produces amotivation — the SDT equivalent of helplessness.

## CRPD Article 12 and the will-and-preferences revolution

The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Article 12 establishes that persons with disabilities enjoy legal capacity on an equal basis with others in all aspects of life. **General Comment No. 1** (2014, CRPD/C/GC/1) represents the most radical interpretation: it maintains that "mental capacity is not, as is commonly presented, an objective, scientific and naturally occurring phenomenon" but "is contingent on social and political contexts," prohibits all substitute decision-making, rejects functional capacity tests as discriminatory, and requires replacing the "best interests" paradigm with a "will and preferences" paradigm.

The standard capacity assessment model (Grisso & Appelbaum, 1998, *Assessing Competence to Consent to Treatment*) evaluates understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and expressing a choice. From a cognitive science perspective, this model has been critiqued as overly focused on individualized cognitive skills while ignoring the relational nature of all human decision-making (Series, 2015, *International Journal of Law and Psychiatry*; Kong, 2017, *Mental Capacity in Relationship*). The CRPD Committee's critique that third parties cannot "accurately assess the inner-workings of the human mind" resonates with the cognitive science evidence on the unreliability of capacity judgments — subject to anchoring, diagnostic overshadowing, and implicit bias.

Bach and Kerzner (2010, Law Commission of Ontario) proposed three decision-making statuses: legally independent, supported decision-making, and facilitated decision-making (using "best interpretation of will and preferences" rather than "best interests"). The minimum threshold they propose — capacity to "express one's intention or will in ways that at least one other person can reasonably describe as meaningful" — is deliberately minimal, shifting the burden from the person demonstrating capacity to supporters demonstrating they have exhausted all means of interpretation. Flynn and Arstein-Kerslake (2014, *International Journal of Law in Context*; 2016, *International Journal of Human Rights*) have been central in developing the theoretical architecture for CRPD-compliant legal capacity reform, while Szmukler (2019, *World Psychiatry*) has offered critical perspectives arguing the Committee's position is insufficiently defined for clinical application.

## Japan's anti-paternalism architecture: Guidelines, contracts, and persistent gaps

Japan ratified the CRPD in January 2014, catalyzing a series of policy reforms. The **「障害福祉サービス等の提供に係る意思決定支援ガイドライン」** (MHLW Decision-Making Support Guidelines), issued March 31, 2017, represents the centerpiece of Japan's supported decision-making framework. The Guidelines establish a three-step priority hierarchy: (1) support the person to make decisions themselves as far as possible (可能な限り本人が自ら意思決定できるよう支援), (2) confirm or estimate the person's will and preferences (意思及び選好を推定), and (3) as a **last resort** (最後の手段), examine "best interests" (最善の利益).

A critical anti-paternalism provision states that **even decisions staff consider irrational must be respected** as long as they do not infringe on others' rights (職員等の価値観においては不合理と思われる決定でも、他者への権利を侵害しないのであれば、その選択を尊重するよう努める). The Guidelines require appointment of a Decision-Making Support Coordinator (意思決定支援責任者), convening of Decision-Making Support Meetings (意思決定支援会議), and integration into service plans. However, MHLW-funded research noted that the concept of 意思決定支援 remained "undifferentiated" (未分化) when the Guidelines were issued, conflating supported decision-making (where the person decides) and proxy decision-making (where a third party decides).

The **準委任契約 (quasi-mandate contract)** framework offers a distinctive Japanese anti-paternalism tool grounded in civil law. Under Articles 643 and 656 of the Civil Code, disability welfare services are legally classifiable as quasi-mandate contracts where the service user is the **mandator** (委任者/principal) who retains authority, and the provider is the **mandatary** (受任者/agent) who owes a duty of care (善管注意義務) but must act within the scope of the principal's will. This structurally positions the user as decision-maker, contrasting sharply with the pre-2003 **措置制度** (administrative measures system) where the government determined service eligibility, content, and provider, and service users were passive recipients.

The shift from 措置制度 to 契約制度 (contract system) via the 2003 支援費制度 (Support Fee System) was explicitly framed as anti-paternalism reform based on normalization principles and self-determination. However, the Japan Federation of Bar Associations cautioned that "individuals who have difficulty with information gathering, understanding, judgment, and communication cannot become equal parties without some form of support" — the reform risked replacing government paternalism with market vulnerability. Comparative policy scholars characterize Japan's disability policy as **"productivist, paternalistic, family-oriented, and firmly involving medical expertise"** in eligibility assessment.

The CRPD Committee's 2022 Concluding Observations on Japan (CRPD/C/JPN/CO/1) expressed deep concern about **the lack of progress in abolishing the guardianship system** — approximately 16,000 persons remained under guardianship. The Committee recommended repealing all discriminatory legal provisions and establishing supported decision-making mechanisms respecting autonomy, will, and preferences. Japan indicated the guardianship system would be reviewed by 2027.

Japanese academic engagement with paternalism is substantial. 樋澤吉彦 (Hizawa Yoshihiko) proposed that "limited paternalism with complete justification requirements is an indispensable principle" for self-determination support. A 2007 paper in 社会福祉学 examined five justification criteria for paternalism, focusing on "autonomy-respecting paternalism" (自律を尊重するパターナリズム), finding that even this concept can operate oppressively due to the polysemy of autonomy. The Paternalism Research Group (パターナリズム研究会), founded at Kokugakuin University in the mid-1980s and holding over 100 meetings across three decades, represents sustained interdisciplinary engagement. Takahashi et al. (2022/2024, *International Journal of Developmental Disabilities*) surveyed social workers and adult guardians in Japan and Taiwan, finding that older practitioners with more experience were more likely to practice supported decision-making, though the 2017 Guidelines had not yet been fully operationalized.

## Empirical evidence: What works against paternalism

Measurement of paternalistic attitudes relies on instruments including the **ATTID** (Attitudes Toward Intellectual Disability Questionnaire; Morin et al., 2013), the **PACA** (Paternalist/Autonomist Care Assessment; Fernández-Ballesteros et al., 2019), and disability-specific IAT adaptations. Assouline et al. (2024, *Journal of Applied Research in Intellectual Disabilities*) found that among 157 direct support professionals and 38 managers, **managers held more paternalistic attitudes** than frontline staff, while more work experience correlated with less paternalistic attitudes — suggesting paternalism may be partly a function of organizational distance from service users.

**Outcomes research** strongly favors empowerment approaches. Wehmeyer and Schwartz (1996) found that higher self-determination predicted membership in higher quality-of-life groups among adults with intellectual disability. Lachapelle et al. replicated this finding across Canada, the US, Belgium, and France. Wehmeyer et al. (2012, *Exceptional Children*; 2013, *Journal of Special Education*) established the first **causal relationship** between self-determination interventions and enhanced self-determination outcomes using group-randomized designs. Burke et al.'s (2020, *Remedial and Special Education*) meta-analysis confirmed effectiveness across grade levels, disability labels, and settings.

The most direct **cognitive intervention study** is Sánchez-Izquierdo et al. (2019, *Clinical Interventions in Aging*), a quasi-experimental study with 118 professional caregivers. Three weekly group sessions using modeling, role-playing, and reinforcement produced significantly lower paternalistic appraisals at post-test and 14-week follow-up, plus greater autonomist behaviors. However, paternalistic **behaviors** proved harder to change than **attitudes** — a finding consistent with the implicit bias literature showing that explicit attitude change does not automatically transfer to behavior. Active Support training has the strongest evidence base as a staff-level intervention, with meta-analysis finding moderate-to-large effect sizes for staff assistance quality (d = 0.56 to d = 1.03; Totsika et al., 2010/2020), though the sole US RCT (Qian et al., 2019) found no significant effects due to implementation challenges.

On **cost-effectiveness**, the UK Individual Budgets evaluation (Glendinning et al., 2008) — one of very few RCTs in adult social care — found individual budgets were cost-effective for social care outcomes with little overall cost difference from conventional services. The systematic review by Fleming et al. (2019, *Campbell Systematic Reviews*) found individualized funding can produce positive outcomes for choice, control, and quality of life. SROI analyses of disability-inclusive enterprises show returns of **$1.47–$2.65 per dollar invested** over three years, rising to **$8.48–$12.63** over twenty years (Hutchinson et al., 2024, Centre for Social Impact).

The **capability approach** introduces important theoretical tensions. Claassen (2014, *Economics & Philosophy*) demonstrated that the capability approach "prescribes paternalist government actions to the extent it requires promotion of specific functionings rather than capabilities." Sen consistently refuses to endorse a predetermined capability list to avoid paternalism, while Nussbaum's specific list of ten central capabilities acknowledges some paternalism may be unavoidable. For people with cognitive disabilities, the question of whether to promote capabilities (freedom to function) or functionings (actual functioning) becomes particularly acute — promoting functionings may require precisely the paternalistic intervention that the framework ostensibly opposes.

## Conclusion: Breaking the paternalism-helplessness cycle

The cognitive science evidence reveals paternalism in disability services as a **dual-sided self-reinforcing system** rather than a one-directional imposition. On the professional side, cognitive load forces System 1 processing that activates disability stereotypes; loss aversion and default bias make restrictive decisions cognitively efficient; bounded rationality leads to satisficing with established routines. On the service user side, paternalistic environments create learned helplessness through internal, stable, global attributions; thwarted basic psychological needs produce amotivation; reduced initiative confirms professional assumptions about incapacity.

Three insights emerge from this synthesis that are underrepresented in current literature. First, the **quasi-mandate contract framework** offers a uniquely Japanese contribution to anti-paternalism theory that deserves broader international attention — it provides a legal structure that architecturally positions the service user as principal, rather than relying on professionals' goodwill to cede authority. Second, the **Maier-Seligman revision** of learned helplessness (passivity as default, control as learned) implies that service environments must be actively designed to teach agency — the absence of paternalism is insufficient; positive autonomy support is necessary. Third, the striking **explicit-implicit attitude dissociation** among disability professionals (77.8% implicit bias coexisting with 70.3% explicit neutrality) suggests that conventional training targeting explicit attitudes will fail; interventions must address automatic cognitive processes through environmental redesign, choice architecture modification, and structural accountability mechanisms rather than attitudinal education alone.

The research base contains significant gaps. No studies directly apply prospect theory to disability support worker risk decisions. Nudge theory has not been systematically examined from a disability rights critique perspective. The intersection of cognitive load research with disability care worker populations remains unexplored. Japan-specific empirical research on implementation of the 2017 Guidelines and operationalization of the quasi-mandate contract framework in disability welfare is needed. These gaps represent productive research frontiers where cognitive science, behavioral economics, and disability rights scholarship can generate both theoretical advances and practical interventions.
